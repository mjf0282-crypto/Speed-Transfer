What Actually Happened
Why Your Speed Stayed on the Range
Speed training works by overloading the system — forcing your body to move faster than it's used to. The problem is, that new speed was earned in a controlled, feedback-rich environment with no consequences. The course is the opposite of that.
The Core Issue
Speed training changes your sequencing, tempo, and the feel of a "normal" swing. When you got to the course, your brain reverted to the old pattern — the one that felt safe and familiar under pressure. The new speed hadn't been stress-tested. This is extremely common and completely fixable.
What Trackman Gave You
A Controlled Environment With Every Variable Removed
Same mat. Same target. Instant feedback. No OB. No playing partners. No score. Your nervous system learned to swing fast in a zero-pressure setting — which is totally different from the first tee of a new course.
What the Course Demanded
A New Swing Under Conditions Your Brain Hadn't Seen Yet
Different lie. Real consequences. No data. A score to protect. Under stress, your brain defaults to the pattern it trusts most — the old one. The new, faster swing hadn't earned that trust yet.
Transfer Problem #1
Tempo Mismatch
Speed training typically shortens the backswing or rushes transition. On the course, without Trackman confirming ball speed, you unconsciously "braked" before impact to feel in control.
Transfer Problem #2
No Pressure Rehearsal
The new swing was never tested under any stakes. The range is practice — but practice without simulated pressure doesn't prepare you for real pressure.
Transfer Problem #3
Feel Hasn't Caught Up
The faster swing feels "too much" without the screen confirming it's working. On the course that feeling reads as danger, so you back off. You're not trusting what you've built.
The Good News
The speed is real. It's in there. This program is not about rebuilding anything — it's about convincing your brain that the new swing is safe to use when it matters. That requires structured pressure rehearsal, deliberate feedback removal, and specific on-course protocols. All of which follow.

You don't need more speed work. You need to convince your nervous system the new swing is trustworthy. These three phases do exactly that — progressively, and within a 1–2 session per week schedule.

Wedge Distance Control
The Scoring Zone:
40–120 Yards
Your new speed means longer drives and shorter approaches — but only if your wedge game can convert those opportunities. Distance control inside 120 yards is the most direct path from 12 to single digits. This is where shots are actually saved.
Why Wedge Distance Control Is Different Now
Speed training primarily affects your full swing. But added speed changes your sequencing and tempo throughout the bag — including your wedges. Many players find their partial wedge distances have shifted after a speed block. Before drilling, re-baseline all your wedge distances at the new swing speed. Don't assume your old 80-yard number is still your 80-yard number.

Build Your Wedge Distance Map
Trackman on
Every wedge club
3 swing speeds
Before you can control distance, you need an accurate, current map of what each wedge at each effort level actually carries. Not what you think it carries — what Trackman says it carries. This is your scoring system.
The 3-Speed Matrix
For each wedge (PW, GW, SW, LW): hit 5 balls at 50%, 75%, and full swing. Record average carry at each speed. You now have 12 reliable distances instead of 4. This is your on-course decision tree.
Data Collection
Clock Face Drill
Use arm position as a distance cue: 7 o'clock backswing = short, 9 o'clock = mid, 10 o'clock = full. Hit 10 balls at each position with your SW. Record the carry. Repeat with GW. You're building feel anchors tied to specific distances.
Feel System
Spin Rate Check
With new swing speed, your spin rates may have changed. Check your 100-yard SW spin on Trackman — if it's dropped below 6,500 RPM, you're losing stopping power. Note how this affects where you need to land the ball vs. where you want it to finish.
Trackman
Gap Identification
After logging the matrix, find the distances that don't fit neatly into a club/speed combo. For most players there are 2–3 "awkward" yardages (e.g. 67 yards, 88 yards). These are your practice priorities — not your comfortable distances.

The Protocols That Keep Your Speed Alive
Once you're on the course, your technical brain needs to be completely offline. These are the specific tools — a pre-round warm-up cap, a pre-shot routine, a speed cue, and a post-shot process — that keep the new swing accessible under pressure.

70 balls in the heat before a round will drain you before you reach the 7th hole — exactly what happened at Marine Park. Pre-round warm-up has one job: activate the body and confirm feel for the day. Leave the practice for the weekly session.

What Killed Your Speed Last Trip
Most likely: you got tight on the first tee, took a controlled swing to "find it," hit a mediocre drive, and then spent the rest of the round trying to manage your way around the course on the old swing. The fix is a pre-shot routine that bypasses that protective instinct before it can fire.
Pre-Shot Routine · Your Transfer Mechanism
The 5-Step Process That Unlocks Your Speed
1
Read & Commit to a Target
Not "the fairway" — a specific spot. A tree, a discoloration, a landmark 30 yards wide. The more specific, the less room for doubt. Doubt is what triggers braking.
2
Use Your Updated Yardage Chart
New swing = new distances. Commit to the number. If you're debating between 6i and 7i, take more club and swing freely — a committed 6i beats a tentative 7i every time.
3
Say Your Tempo Cue
Out loud or internally — say the exact cue you built in Phase 1. "Load… fire." "Long… fast." Whatever it is. This is your on-ramp to the new swing. It bypasses the protective override.
4
One Practice Swing That Feels Like the Shot
Not a gentle maintenance swing — the actual speed of the shot you're about to hit. Feel the tempo cue. If it doesn't feel right, reset and try again. Don't step in until the practice swing felt fast.
5
Step In and Pull the Trigger Within 5 Seconds
No more thinking. The decision is made. Your only job is to swing with the same feel as that practice swing. Trust it. The speed is in there — this routine is how you get to it.
When You Brake Mid-Swing
Recognize It. Reset. Don't Chase It.
If you feel yourself decelerate — don't swing harder on the next shot to compensate. That just adds a new variable. Reset your routine. Tempo cue. One practice swing that felt fast. Then go. Braking happens. The routine is the answer.
On Tight Tee Shots
This Is Where It Goes Wrong
Narrow fairways trigger the protective instinct most. Counter it: aim your miss pattern into the safe side of the fairway, then swing at your 85% trust swing — not 70%. The goal is committed, not controlled.
Post-Shot Protocol
  • Fast or braked? — honest internal rating: did you swing at your new speed or back off?
  • If braked: what triggered it? — tight hole, bad lie, bad last shot, playing partner watching? Note the pattern
  • Read the miss by ball flight — start line = face angle; curve = face-to-path; height = contact quality
  • One thought, then move on — either "that's the new swing" (reinforce) or "reset the routine next time" (correct)
  • Never carry a bad shot to the next tee — the new swing doesn't need protecting. It needs repeating
